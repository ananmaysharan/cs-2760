=== start ===
Mark is a marketing manager at 2760 Inc., the hottest marketing agency in NYC.

You go into the office four days a week. You have a corner desk on the 23rd floor.

It's 2025, and you just found out you got promoted to Marketing Manager.

Congratulations. This is exciting stuff.

You are assigned to work on a project for one of your firm's biggest clients: Target.

-> year_2025

=== year_2025 ===
January 31st, 2025.

Your boss pulls you into your first meeting as a manager and gives you the rundown on the yearly plan.

Staff are already using AI quietly for drafts, summaries, and campaign support.

You're overseeing three junior employees this year, plus an intern this summer.

Your boss asks if your team is currently using AI. He encourages you to push your team to adopt AI more into your work.

What approach do you take with your team?

* [Let each person use AI as they see fit, while holding every worker responsible for their final output.]
    You tell your team they can use generative AI for drafting, summarizing notes, and supporting their workflow, but every person remains accountable for what they produce.
    -> year_2026

* [Encourage heavier AI use for summaries, copy edits, and research, and suggest you may not even need a summer intern.]
    You push your team to use generative AI for meeting notes, small edits, and research tasks, and you openly suggest the intern role may no longer be necessary.
    -> year_2026

* [Allow AI use, but raise concerns about employee expertise and the firm's intellectual property.]
    You say your team can use AI tools as they see fit, but you also voice concerns about employees losing expertise and the company feeding its intellectual property into a black-box system.
    -> year_2026

=== year_2026 ===
January 31st, 2026.

Your agency rolls out new AI productivity tools that can track how much time employees spend prompting, editing, and producing content.

Leadership says the data will help identify the most efficient workflows.

How do you respond?

* [Allow the tools, but only as one input among many.]
    You allow the tools to be used, but you tell your team that while efficiency matters, good marketing still requires judgment, experimentation, and ideas that cannot always be captured by a dashboard.
    -> year_2027

* [Use the analytics to reward speed and output.]
    You encourage your team to use the analytics to improve speed and output, and you start rewarding workflows that produce the most content in the least amount of time, even if the work becomes more standardized.
    -> year_2027

* [Push back on using AI productivity data as a measure of creative value.]
    You argue that constant tracking will narrow the work, increase pressure, and make it harder for employees to develop their own judgment and style.
    -> year_2027

=== year_2027 ===
January 31st, 2027.

You are at a global all-hands meeting with all the managers at the company.

It's your first time at such a meeting.

An economic downturn hits, and the CEO announces a hiring freeze and a drive to reduce headcount, arguing that agentic AI tools are capable of producing junior-level work.

After the meeting, your boss asks you to review headcount on your team. Your junior employees have already heard rumors and are worried about their job security.

What do you say to your boss after the review?

* [Argue that your team's headcount should stay stable because AI-driven output still needs junior staff to support fast-turnaround production.]
    You argue that increased output and productivity from the team's growing AI usage still requires both you and your junior employees to keep production moving quickly, especially to clean up errors in AI-generated work.
    -> year_2028

* [Agree that agentic AI can replace part of the team and recommend cutting your least effective junior employee.]
    You agree with your boss that agentic AI tools are fully capable of junior-level work, and you conclude that firing your least effective junior employee would raise team revenue by 10% while keeping output at the same level.
    -> year_2028

* [Refuse to reduce headcount and argue for redesigning junior roles.]
    You refuse to reduce headcount, arguing instead that junior employees should take on new responsibilities like AI coordination, quality review, audience insight, and brand safety so they still have a path into the field as the work changes.
    -> year_2028

=== year_2028 ===
January 31st, 2028.

You are producing more output than ever as you take on multiple clients and your team leverages AI systems to create more content.

People around the company are talking about your team.

At the same time, the sheer amount of content is making it harder to evaluate campaign results qualitatively the way you used to.

You need a data scientist to do comprehensive quantitative analysis, but the budget is tight and there is a hiring freeze.

Every other firm is doing custom data analysis for campaigns, and your boss says you need to do the same work on the same budget.

You've also heard that junior-level managers may be cut in the next round of layoffs, and cutting budget could put you in line for a promotion.

What do you do?

* [Tell your boss the work is too complex and you need a real data scientist.]
    You tell your boss that the data science behind these campaigns is more complicated than ever and that your team is already stretched designing targeted work. You argue that bringing in a specialist will pay for itself by keeping campaigns effective.
    -> ending

* [Say the team can handle analytics with ChatGPT instead of hiring.]
    You tell your boss the team does not need more tools because, even without data science expertise, everyone has access to ChatGPT and can quickly generate custom analytics. It adds more work for the team, but you frame it as manageable.
    -> ending

* [Argue for hiring a data scientist even if it means short-term sacrifice.]
    You argue that if you do not hire a data scientist, your team's total output will become less creative and likely less profitable. You propose cutting the team's ad budget in the short term and tell your boss you should train one of the junior staff to specialize in AI marketing, with paid hours during the workday for workers and managers to master AI principles, prompting, evaluation, and responsible use.
    -> ending

=== ending ===
Four years in, you've helped shape how AI changes the work, the team, and the kind of manager you become.
